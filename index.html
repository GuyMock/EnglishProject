Paper 1 • Visual Texts

Focus on purpose, audience, visual language, and how design choices shape meaning. Cards reveal as you scroll. Hover for tilt.

Elephant Turning into Sand

  • Purpose: Desertification harms wildlife
  • Audience: Environment conscious viewers
  • Style: Fade to dust, spotlight subject
  • Big idea: Urgency and loss
  • Techniques: Photorealism, blending, soft light

Golfer with an Axe

  • Purpose: Critique deforestation for leisure
  • Audience: Golfers, developers
  • Style: Calm scene with a jarring swap
  • Big idea: Hidden costs of fun
  • Techniques: Irony, juxtaposition, contrast

Winter Collection Girl

  • Purpose: Expose child poverty through fashion codes
  • Audience: Fashion industry and public
  • Style: Studio minimalism
  • Big idea: Trend masks suffering
  • Techniques: Irony, direct gaze, restraint

Weight Watchers Doors

  • Purpose: Visualize slimming result
  • Audience: Weight loss seekers
  • Style: Clean architectural pun
  • Big idea: Before and after in one frame
  • Techniques: Minimalism, proportion play

Chupa Chups Sugar-Free

  • Purpose: Signal zero sugar
  • Audience: Parents, kids, health minded
  • Style: Realism with a twist
  • Big idea: Even ants pass by
  • Techniques: Humor, symbolism, muted palette

Glasses, See Clearly

  • Purpose: Clarity sells the product
  • Audience: People needing vision help
  • Style: Real detail inside lens
  • Big idea: Hidden detail unlocked
  • Techniques: Depth of field, focal contrast

Individual Oral

Flow

  1. Choose a global issue
  2. Select one literary and one non-literary text
  3. Analyze how each constructs the issue
  4. Synthesize, conclude audience impact

Timing

  • 10 minutes presentation
  • 5 minutes Q&A

Checklist

  • Clear guiding question
  • Methods and effects named
  • Specific evidence
  • Cohesive structure

Text Selection Guidelines 2025–2027

Teacher: Pauline Westwood

What counts as a work

  • 1 single literary text such as a novel, autobiography, or biography
  • 2 or more short literary texts such as a novella
  • 5–10 short stories
  • 5–8 essays
  • 10–15 letters
  • A long poem over 600 lines or 15–20 poems

Poetry Drama Prose: fiction Prose: non-fiction

Glossary

Key terms with definitions for quick reference. Add more words whenever needed.

WordMeaning
AllegoryA story, image, or text that can be interpreted to reveal a hidden meaning, often moral or political.George Orwell’s "Animal Farm" as an allegory for the Russian Revolution.
AlliterationRepetition of initial consonant sounds."Peter Piper picked a peck of pickled peppers."
AllusionIndirect reference to another text, person, or event."He’s a real Romeo with the ladies."
AnaphoraRepetition of a word or phrase at the start of successive clauses."I have a dream… I have a dream…"
AnecdoteA short, personal story used to illustrate a point.A teacher telling a story about failing a test to encourage students.
AntithesisContrasting ideas placed together."It was the best of times, it was the worst of times."
ArchetypeA universal symbol or character type.The "hero’s journey" in myths and movies.
AssonanceRepetition of vowel sounds."Hear the mellow wedding bells."
ColloquialismInformal or conversational language."What’s up?" instead of "How are you?"
ConnotationThe associations or feelings a word carries."Home" suggests warmth and comfort beyond its literal meaning.
DenotationThe literal dictionary definition of a word."Snake" meaning a reptile.
DictionThe author’s choice of words.Formal diction: "The gentleman spoke with dignity."
EllipsisThree dots (…) indicating omission or pause."So… what now?"
EpistropheRepetition of words at the end of successive clauses."See no evil, hear no evil, speak no evil."
EthosAppeal to credibility or character.A doctor endorsing a medical product.
PathosAppeal to emotion.A charity ad showing images of suffering children.
LogosAppeal to logic or reason.Using statistics to support an argument.
Figurative LanguageLanguage that is not literal."The classroom was a zoo."
HyperboleDeliberate exaggeration."I’ve told you a million times."
ImageryDescriptive language appealing to the senses."The golden sunset spilled across the horizon."
IronyA contrast between expectation and reality.A fire station burns down.
JuxtapositionPlacing two ideas/images close together for contrast.A wealthy mansion next to a slum.
MetaphorComparison without using "like" or "as"."Time is a thief."
SimileComparison using "like" or "as"."Her smile was as bright as the sun."
MotifA recurring element or symbol in a text.Darkness recurring in "Macbeth".
Onomatopoeia/OnomatopeicWords that imitate sounds."Bang," "buzz," "splash."
OxymoronTwo contradictory terms combined."Deafening silence."
ParadoxA statement that seems contradictory but reveals truth."Less is more."
ParallelismGrammatical repetition for rhythm or balance."Easy come, easy go."
ParodyImitation with exaggeration for comic effect."Scary Movie" parodying horror films.
PersonificationGiving human qualities to non-human things."The wind whispered through the trees."
PolysyndetonRepetition of conjunctions."We ate and laughed and sang and danced."
AsyndetonOmission of conjunctions."I came, I saw, I conquered."
ProlepsisForeshadowing or anticipation of future events."He was a dead man walking."
Rhetorical QuestionA question asked for effect, not an answer."Who cares?"
SatireHumor, irony, or exaggeration to criticize."Animal Farm" satirizing politics.
SyntaxThe arrangement of words and phrases.Yoda: "Powerful you have become."
RegisterThe level of formality in language.Formal register in a courtroom vs. casual register with friends.
ToneThe writer’s attitude toward the subject.Sarcastic, hopeful, melancholic.
VoiceThe personality or style of the speaker/writer.Hemingway’s sparse voice vs. Faulkner’s elaborate one.
Declarative SentenceMakes a statement."The sky is blue."
Interrogative SentenceAsks a question."Is the sky blue?"
Imperative SentenceGives a command."Look at the sky."
Exclamatory SentenceExpresses strong emotion."What a beautiful sky!"
Standard English (SE)Formal, grammatically correct English used in education and media."I am going to the store."
Received Pronunciation (RP)The accent traditionally considered "standard" British English.Queen’s English.
IdiolectAn individual’s unique way of speaking.Shakespeare’s personal style of wordplay.
SociolectLanguage style associated with a social group.Teen slang: "That’s lit!"
DialectA regional form of language.Scottish English: "wee" for "small."
Code-SwitchingShifting between languages/registers depending on context.A bilingual speaker moving between Spanish and English mid-sentence.
ConsonanceRepetition of consonant sounds within words."Pitter-patter."
RhymeRepetition of similar sounds at the ends of words."Cat" / "hat".
RhythmThe pattern of stressed and unstressed syllables."Shall I compare thee to a summer’s day?"
MeterStructured rhythmic pattern in poetry.Iambic pentameter: "Shall I comPARE thee TO a SUMmer’s DAY?"
CaesuraA pause in the middle of a line of poetry."To be, or not to be — that is the question."
EnjambmentSentence continues across lines of poetry."The sun rises / over the hill."
Free VersePoetry without a set rhyme or rhythm.Walt Whitman’s "Leaves of Grass."
SymbolismWhen an object, character, or event represents a larger idea.A dove symbolizing peace.
ThemeThe central idea or underlying message of a text.Love and fate in "Romeo and Juliet".
Thesis StatementThe main argument or claim of an essay."Technology improves human life despite its risks."
Concluding SentenceA sentence that wraps up a paragraph or argument."Therefore, the evidence shows that climate change is human-driven."
TabloidsNewspapers that focus on sensational stories and celebrity news."A tabloid headline: 'Shocking Secret Revealed About Celebrity!'"
BroadsheetsNewspapers that focus on serious reporting and detailed analysis."A broadsheet headline: 'Government Introduces New Climate Policy to Cut Emissions by 2030.'"

Course Design Checklist

  • Minimum 4 SL works or 6 HL works and matching number of bodies of work
  • At least 1 SL or 2 HL works from PRL in the studied language
  • At least 1 SL or 2 HL works in translation from PRL
  • At least 2 free choice works allowed, may be in translation
  • At least 1 SL or 2 HL works per Area of Exploration
  • Cover 2 SL or 3 HL literary forms, periods, and regions; at least 2 continents
  • Enough bodies of work to support IO and HL Essay
