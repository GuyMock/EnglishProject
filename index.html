What I should do before I start writing

This is the routine I want to follow so my work feels controlled instead of rushed. It keeps the planning practical and stops me from drifting into description.

1

Annotate first

  • Mark techniques and the message they help create.
  • Label annotations as paragraph 1, 2, or 3.
  • Spot patterns, not random one-off details.
2

Build the argument

  • Move beyond literal meaning.
  • Frame ideas as a critique, commentary, or exploration.
  • Track how the text manipulates the audience.
3

Write with control

  • Use literary and linguistic terminology naturally.
  • Keep the tone academic but still sounding like me.
  • Do not rush the ending or begin a new point too late.

Read the task Annotate Group ideas Write a thesis Use PEE or PEEL Link back to purpose or global issue End strongly

If the teacher says there are 30 seconds left, I should not start a new text or a new paragraph. I should close the answer properly.

Poster This Friday

Poster analysis I can use fast under pressure

The key is to start with the most dominant feature, then explain how the design choices direct the viewer toward the message.

Introduction formula

  1. Text type
  2. Content in one clean line
  3. Writer, artist, or brand
  4. Context and where it appears
  5. Tone
  6. Purpose
  7. Audience
  8. Message

Body paragraph structure

  1. Point: make a claim about a feature.
  2. Example: describe exactly what is visible.
  3. Explanation: analyse method, effect, and audience response.
  4. Link: connect back to purpose or message.

What to notice first

  • The most prominent feature in the frame
  • The focal point and where the eye lands first
  • The gaze of the subject, if there is a person
  • How side details create ambience without taking focus

Colour and contrast

  • Colour palette and the mood it creates
  • Contrast between text and background
  • Contrast between subject and setting
  • Whether colours symbolise danger, calm, innocence, urgency, or luxury

Text and typography

  • Font style, size, spacing, and graphic weight
  • Capital letters, bold text, and slogans
  • How punctuation creates pause, urgency, or emphasis
  • How the text design matches the wider message

Propaganda and persuasion toolkit

  • Emotive language: duty, glory, sacrifice, honour
  • Rhetorical question: the answer is already implied
  • Peer pressure: everyone else seems to be involved
  • Endorsement: famous or respected figures support the message
  • Emotional blackmail: guilt is used to force agreement
  • Hero imagery: the audience is invited to imagine themselves inside the role

Starter I can adapt

In this poster, [surname or brand] uses a dominant focal point, deliberate colour contrast, and carefully placed text to guide the viewer toward the message that [core idea].

Everything on a poster is there for a reason, so I should treat every detail as deliberate.

Paper 1

Visual and non-literary analysis without overexplaining

My job is not to describe everything in the image. My job is to explain how selected choices shape the message and position the audience.

CFLCPGF photo framework

  • C: Composition
  • F: Focus or focal point
  • L: Light
  • C: Colour
  • P: People or place
  • G: Gaze
  • F: Framing or cropping

Cartoon question bank

  • What is the main issue and what is the context?
  • What is the main message about that issue?
  • Who are the central people and how are they represented?
  • What do the background and composition add?
  • How effective is the cartoon and why?

Cartoon techniques

  • Colour, symbols, labels, and speech bubbles
  • Facial expression, angle, scale, and proportion
  • Emanata, gutters, panels, frames, and bleed
  • Caricature and graphic weight

Safer interpretation beats wild guessing

I should start with what is explicitly visible, then move to what it implies, and only then connect it to the bigger message or context. That keeps the analysis credible.

  • Avoid guessing exact age, motives, jealousy, or private thoughts.
  • Avoid labelling people in my own voice.
  • If harsh language appears in the text, discuss the register and its effect instead.
  • Keep claims grounded in evidence from the image or wording.

Turn weak comments into stronger analysis

Weak

The girl wants to be like the doll and is jealous.

Better

The contrast between the clothed doll and the unclothed doll suggests a tension between innocence and adult performance, which unsettles the viewer and exposes how gender expectations are being learned too early.

Visual analysis language

  • positions the viewer
  • constructs sympathy
  • provokes discomfort
  • satirises social expectations
  • reinforces the central critique
  • normalises, challenges, exposes, or undercuts

Photo analysis reminders

  • Rule of thirds can make the image feel deliberate and balanced.
  • Foreground and background often create social or emotional contrast.
  • Black-and-white photography can intensify starkness and seriousness.
  • Juxtaposition is one of the fastest ways to create a message.

Punctuation and syntax

  • Ellipsis can create a pause or a feeling of trailing thought.
  • A dash can signal interruption or hesitation.
  • Contractions can make a voice feel informal or intimate.
  • A short sentence can land like a punchline or warning.

Paper 2

Comparative writing that feels arguable, not descriptive

Strong Paper 2 writing comes from patterns, comparisons, and purpose. I need to move beyond retelling and show how the writers build meaning differently.

Essay architecture

  • Open with a comparative thesis, not a summary.
  • Make paragraph one the strongest.
  • Keep paragraph two a little lighter.
  • End paragraph three with power.

Move beyond the literal

  • Frame the text as a critique, commentary, or exploration.
  • Ask what the writer wants the audience to think or feel.
  • Push from surface detail to wider implication.

Patterns matter most

  • Repeated imagery reinforces a central idea.
  • Juxtapositions highlight conflict.
  • Tonal shifts show development or tension.
  • Structure can reveal control, collapse, or change.

Zoom in, then zoom out

  • Zoom in on one phrase, image, or structural choice.
  • Analyse the method closely.
  • Zoom out to show how it connects to the whole work or global context.
  • Do not leave the quote floating on its own.

Authorial intent and audience

  • Consider how the reader is guided, manipulated, or unsettled.
  • Use phrases like positions the reader or constructs sympathy.
  • Do not overdo context, but use it when it strengthens the claim.
  • Short, well-analysed quotes beat long quotations every time.

Sentence stems I can actually use

The lexical choice of [word] suggests...

This pattern reinforces the wider critique of...

Rather than simply presenting [idea], the writer exposes...

The contrast between [detail] and [detail] positions the reader to...

By zooming in on [method], the text develops the broader idea that...

Both writers explore [theme], but they do so through different tonal and structural choices.

Oral And IO

What I need to say, and how I need to sound

The oral works best when the structure is planned, the vocabulary is controlled, and the final sentence feels deliberate instead of rushed.

Oral structure

  1. Start with text type, writer, context, and argument.
  2. Talk about the verses, not just the general idea.
  3. Use the chorus to track repetition or tonal shift.
  4. Use the outro to show what remains with the listener.
  5. Finish with a dynamic final sentence.

Theme, tone, mood

  • Theme: the central idea or concern
  • Tone: the speaker or writer's attitude
  • Mood: the feeling created in the audience
  • Use all three, but keep them distinct

Delivery reminders

  • Slow down and let the analysis breathe.
  • No fillers like um, yeah, or cool.
  • Do not invent vocabulary I would never actually say.
  • The examiner should clearly know when I have finished.

IO global issue checklist

  • It should matter on a wide scale.
  • It should be transnational, not tied to one tiny setting.
  • Its impact should be felt across different contexts.
  • It must genuinely appear in both texts.

Possible global issue: The enduring desire for human connection.

Fields of inquiry I can draw from

Power, politics, and justice Belonging and otherness Identity and social expectation Culture and community Creativity and resistance

IO pairing idea

Literary text: The Smiths, There Is a Light That Never Goes Out

Non-literary body of work: Liza Donnelly cartoons about gender, belonging, fear, and social expectation

  • The song presents connection as something intense, risky, and deeply desired.
  • Donnelly shows how social scripts can isolate people even inside everyday life.
  • That gives me a shared issue without forcing the texts to be identical.

What I should remember in the IO

  • Quote once only, then analyse it properly.
  • Use surname only when referring to writers.
  • Refer to the global issue more than once, but do not force it every sentence.
  • A body of work means more than one image.

Text Bank

Useful reminders for texts, writers, and bodies of work

This is not for biography dumping. It is for storing the details that help me build better analysis.

Lyric

The Smiths - There Is a Light That Never Goes Out

  • Theme: longing, escape, and the need for closeness.
  • Tone: tender, dramatic, confessional.
  • Useful methods: repetition, hyperbole, journey imagery, motif of light.
  • Strong angle: danger is romanticised because connection matters more than safety.

Lyric

The Smiths - Meat Is Murder

  • Theme: violence, ethics, and moral responsibility.
  • Tone: accusatory, disturbing, confrontational.
  • Useful methods: sensory imagery, imperative force, moral shock.
  • Strong angle: discomfort is the method, not an accident.

Cartoons

Liza Donnelly

  • Humour is used as a tool for social criticism and change.
  • Repeated concerns: gender expectation, belonging, tokenism, fear of womanhood.
  • Useful methods: caption-image contrast, satire, imperative lists, reversal.
  • Strong angle: the cartoons make familiar pressure look absurd.

Poetry

Philip Larkin - Mr Bleaney and related notes

  • Key ideas: routine, limitation, emptiness, and ordinary loneliness.
  • Useful methods: injected dialogue, listing, flat diction, ambiguity.
  • Strong angle: the room can feel almost like a quiet coffin or social trap.
  • Reminder: biography can help, but the poem itself should stay central.

Photography

Robert Capa and war photography

  • Key ideas: immediacy, human cost, danger, dignity under pressure.
  • Useful methods: framing, frozen motion, black-and-white contrast, proximity.
  • Strong angle: photographs do not just document war, they force emotional response.
  • Reminder: talk about composition and viewer effect, not only historical facts.

Drama

Shakespeare - Romeo and Juliet

  • Talk about it as a play: actors, audience, performance, staging.
  • Key concerns: conflict, masculinity, banter, public violence, private feeling.
  • Useful methods: innuendo, dramatic irony, soliloquy, oxymoron, sonnet form.
  • Reminder: Juliet should still feel young, and the Nurse matters as a maternal presence.

Propaganda

First World War posters

  • Context: recruitment and persuasion before modern social media.
  • Useful methods: capital letters, uniforms, direct address, guilt, heroism.
  • Strong angle: propaganda does not just inform; it manipulates behaviour.
  • Reminder: British athletes or young men can be the implied audience.

Checklist

Final reminders before I submit or speak

These are the habits that keep my work sharp and stop easy mistakes costing marks.

Always do

  • Use surname only for writers and authors.
  • State the text type in the introduction.
  • Use short quotes and analyse them properly.
  • Link every paragraph back to message, purpose, or global issue.
  • Plan the final sentence before I start.

Never do

  • No slang, no cliches, no lazy filler words.
  • Do not state the obvious and stop there.
  • Do not list techniques without analysing effect.
  • Do not force statistics into an oral that needs textual evidence.
  • Do not sound fake by using vocabulary that is not really mine.

Punctuation range

  • comma
  • colon
  • semicolon
  • dash
  • question mark
  • exclamation mark
  • brackets
  • ellipsis
  • full stop

Use variety naturally. The point is control, not forcing punctuation for the sake of it.

Final line reminder

A strong ending should sound earned. It should return to the task, the message, and the wider significance in one clean sentence, not drift away.
