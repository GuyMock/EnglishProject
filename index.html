Case study, Liza Donnelly — humor for change

Core ideas

  • Gender expectations, fear of womanhood, pressure to conform
  • Imperatives in culture, "Eat this, wear this, do this, buy this, want this"
  • Media saturation, images that teach roles early
  • Home as first imprint, colour coding like pink as a signal
  • Drawing as voice, approval without speaking
  • Reversal captions, subvert common sayings for effect
  • One laugh at a time, small shifts add up

Methods and effects

  • Juxtaposition: Exaggerated character beside plain crowd, shows what stands out
  • Hyperbole: Big hair or big bag as pressure made visible
  • Irony: "I want to look like X, but not seem like X", image vs identity
  • Intertext: Public figures used as mirrors for trends
  • Register shifts: Demotic captions for direct connection
  • Visual contrast: Dark lines on pale ground, fast read, strong focus
  • Punchline timing: Short line after a setup image, surprise drives the point

Clean PEEL, ready to paste

Point: Donnelly uses irony to expose how culture scripts girls before they can choose.

Example: A child echoes adult commands, "Eat this, wear this, do this, buy this, want this", while a pink room signals a preset role.

Explanation: The imperatives and colour code work together. The list sounds like marketing, the pink palette codes gender. The reader sees how early and how often the script is taught.

Link: By making us laugh and notice the script, the cartoon nudges change, one laugh at a time.

Safety on tone

Do not label subjects with insults. Describe what the image does. If a caption uses casual or harsh language, name the register and the effect on the reader. Keep analysis neutral, precise, and kind.

Thesis

The song turns risk into a tender wish to show how far isolation can push the need to belong. It uses a confessional voice, night drive imagery, anaphora, and a light motif that suggests safety.

Verse 1

  • Method: Anaphora of "take me out" builds urgency
  • Voice: First person confession adds intimacy
  • Effect: The speaker seeks escape and witness

Chorus

  • Image: Night drive, empty streets, borrowed freedom
  • Symbol: Light as acceptance, not just sight
  • Effect: Desire reframed as calm inevitability

Key evidence

"And if a double decker bus crashes into us, to die by your side is such a heavenly way to die."

Method: Hyperbole, irony, romantic fatalism. Effect: Danger turned into union. Link: Connection outweighs fear.

  • Annotate first, plan, then write

  • End with a clear final line that answers the question
  • Name methods and effects, then audience impact

Notes

Image analysis, guidance

Keep claims grounded in what is visible. Avoid guessing age or motives. Use neutral terms. If a text uses offensive language, name the register and explain the effect, but do not repeat it in your own voice.

  • Gaze: Where the subject looks, how it directs us
  • Setting: Props and light frame the subject
  • Contrast: Foreground vs background, warm vs cool colours
  • Symbols: Common cultural links a reader would share
  • Language: If captions use demotic language, log the register, purpose, and impact

Rewrite example, safe academic voice

Before: Labels the subject and assumes inner motives.

After: The boy's doll has no clothes while the girl's doll is dressed. This contrast draws attention to how the image plays with ideas of innocence and exposure. The choice may be designed to unsettle the viewer and raise questions about how children learn gendered norms. The direct gaze and the unicorn poster create tension between child like decor and mature themes.
